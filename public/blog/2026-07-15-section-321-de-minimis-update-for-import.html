Section 321 De Minimis Update for Importers

July 15, 2026 · Estimated reading: 6–8 minutes

Section 321 allows duty-free entry for qualifying shipments under $800. Recent enforcement focus means importers must verify eligibility, documentation, and PGA requirements before claiming the exemption.

Why this matters now

Under 19 U.S.C. § 1321 and 19 CFR 141.81, shipments with a fair retail value not exceeding $800 may enter the US free of duty and without formal entry. CBP treats Section 321 as an administrative simplification, not a blanket safe harbor. In 2026, CBP continues to target undervaluation, split shipments, and prohibited goods that enter under the de minimis stream.

The de minimis threshold was increased from $200 to $800 in 2016. No further statutory increase has been enacted, but oversight bills have been proposed in Congress. Importers should not assume the threshold will remain constant in future sessions.

Current compliance requirements

Eligibility. The shipment must be a single invoice, not part of a larger shipment that has been split to stay under the threshold. CBP looks at the transaction, not just the carton weight or number of packages. Aggregation rules apply when the same buyer orders related items from the same seller within a short period.

Documentation. Even though formal entry is not required, the importer of record must be able to produce, upon request, a commercial invoice or receipt that shows the fair retail value, description of goods, country of origin, and seller information. Lack of adequate documentation is a common cause of de minimis denials and penalties.

PGA holds. Duty-free does not mean review-free. The FDA still enforces prior notice for food, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices under 21 CFR 1.282. The USDA enforces APHIS and phytosanitary requirements under 7 CFR 319. The CPSC may inspect children's products. If a PGA blocks the shipment, the de minimis advantage is lost.

AD/CVD exposure. Section 321 entries are not exempt from antidumping and countervailing duties. If the HTS chapter is subject to an AD/CVD finding, the exemption does not shield the importer from assessment. Verify Chapter 1–99 status before booking under the $800 route.

ISF. If merchandise is shipped by ocean and loaded in a container, an ISF may still be required under 19 CFR 141.84 regardless of the Section 321 status. Split or split-bulk shipments to avoid ISF filing penalties are high-risk patterns that CBP targets.

Common errors importers make

  • Undervaluation: Declaring a lower price to qualify. CBP cross-checks with marketplace listings.
  • Artificial splitting: Multiple orders from the same supplier timed to each fall under $800.
  • Missing country of origin: Every Section 321 entry must be marked or supported by an invoice showing origin.
  • Ignoring PGA rules: Food, seeds, electronics with batteries, and children's toys still require certifications, prior notices, or testing.
  • Skipping reasonable care: The importer of record must still exercise reasonable care in determining admissibility under 19 CFR Part 171.
